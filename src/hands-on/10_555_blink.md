# 10 · 555 timer blink (an oscillator, no code)

## Overview

Before a microcontroller blinks an LED in software, it's worth blinking one with *pure
hardware* — so you appreciate what the chip is doing for you. The **555 timer** is the most
famous IC ever made: a few [resistors](../electronics/resistance.md) and a
[capacitor](../electronics/capacitors.md) around it make a self-running square-wave
[oscillator](../electronics/oscillators.md). You'll build an **astable** blinker, set its
rate with R and C (the same τ = RC from [rung 05](05_rc_fade.md)), and see your first signal
that changes on its own — the perfect bridge into the microcontroller phase.

```
  555 astable (pin numbers for the DIP-8):

         ┌───●───┐ Vcc(8)
        [R1]    [Reset(4) → Vcc]
         │
    (7)Dis─┤        (3) OUT ──[330Ω]──►|── GND   (blinking LED)
        [R2]
         │
   (6)Thr─┼──(2)Trig
         │
        ===C
         │
        GND(1)
```

## What you'll need

From **Stage A** of the [shopping list](README.md):

- A 555 timer IC (NE555), an LED + 330 Ω
- Two resistors (R1, R2 — e.g. 10 kΩ each) and a capacitor (e.g. 10 µF)
- Breadboard, multimeter, 5 V source

## The build

Wire the classic astable (pin map above): R1 from Vcc to discharge(7), R2 from 7 to
threshold(6)+trigger(2), the cap from there to GND. Tie reset(4) to Vcc. LED + resistor on
output(3) to GND.

```
  Blink rate (astable):
    t_high = 0.693 × (R1 + R2) × C
    t_low  = 0.693 ×  R2        × C
    f      = 1.44 / ((R1 + 2·R2) × C)

  For R1 = R2 = 10 kΩ, C = 10 µF:
    f ≈ 1.44 / (30 000 × 0.00001) = 4.8 Hz   (a visible fl* flicker)
  Use C = 100 µF → ≈0.5 Hz, a slow, clear blink.
```

Swap the cap or resistors and watch the rate change — you're tuning an oscillator with the
same RC timing you metered back in rung 05.

## It works when…

- [ ] The LED blinks on its own with no code and no moving parts.
- [ ] Changing R or C changes the blink rate in the direction the formula predicts.
- [ ] (Scope, optional) Pin 3 shows a clean square wave; pin 6 shows the cap's RC ramp.

## What's happening

Inside the 555, two comparators watch the capacitor voltage against ⅓ and ⅔ of Vcc. The cap
charges through R1+R2 until it hits ⅔ Vcc (output flips low, discharge pin drains it through
R2), then discharges to ⅓ Vcc (output flips high, charging resumes) — forever. That's an
[oscillator](../electronics/oscillators.md): an [RC](../electronics/capacitors.md) timing
element plus a threshold-and-flip mechanism. A microcontroller's blink does the same thing
with a hardware [timer](../embedded/timers.md) counting clock ticks instead of an RC ramp —
more precise and reprogrammable, which is exactly why you'll switch to it next in
[rung 11](11_firmware_blink.md).

## Pitfalls

- **Floating reset pin (4)** — must be tied HIGH (to Vcc) or the timer stays held in reset and won't oscillate. A very common "why is it dead" cause.
- **Wrong capacitor polarity** — an electrolytic timing cap must have − to GND. Backwards skews timing or pops the cap.
- **Expecting a perfect 50% duty cycle** — the basic astable's high time is always longer than its low time (charge path includes R1). A diode trick or a different topology evens it out; not needed here.
