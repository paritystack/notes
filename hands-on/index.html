Hands-On Electronics

Overview

This section is the doing counterpart to the Electronics theory pages. Where that section explains how resistance, capacitors, transistors, and power supplies work, this one walks you through building real circuits — one small evening-sized win at a time — until you can design, route, and assemble your own PCB. It’s written for someone comfortable with software/firmware but new to hardware: the hardware is the new thing, so the code stays deliberately simple and every step ends with something that visibly works.

Each rung links back to the theory page it puts into practice, and forward to the Embedded concepts (GPIO, PWM, ADC, power management) you’ll lean on once a microcontroller joins the breadboard.

The capstone

Everything ladders toward one device: a battery-powered sensor node — it reads a sensor over I²C, shows or logs the result, runs from a LiPo cell, sleeps to save power, and lives on a PCB you designed around a bare ATmega328P. Optionally it sends its readings over WiFi via an ESP32 module. By the time you build it, every part of it will be something you’ve already done in isolation.

How to use this section

  • One rung per session. Each project is sized for a single evening (~1–2 h) and ends with a clear “it works when…” check. Don’t skip ahead — each build assumes the last.
  • Prototype on an Arduino Nano clone, then graduate the capstone to a bare ATmega328P on your own board. Same chip, so nothing you learn is wasted.
  • Low voltage only. Everything here stays ≤ 24 V DC (USB 5 V, LiPo 3.7 V, the odd 12 V fan). No mains. No exceptions — see Switches & Relays for why.
  • Measure everything. Get into the habit of confirming with the multimeter what you think is happening. This is the firmware-debugging instinct applied to hardware.
