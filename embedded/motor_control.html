Motor Control

Overview

Motor control is where an MCU stops just reading sensors and starts moving the physical world — and it ties together more peripherals than almost any other embedded task: PWM to drive the power stage, timers for precise switching and dead-time, ADC for current/position sensing, DMA to stream samples, interrupts for the control loop, and DSP math for the transforms. This page maps the landscape — brushed DC, stepper, and BLDC/PMSM motors — and the control techniques from simple PWM speed control up to Field-Oriented Control (FOC), the algorithm behind quiet, efficient drones, e-bikes, and appliances.

BLDC and PMSM are physically the same hardware (3-phase permanent-magnet); the difference is how you drive them — 6-step trapezoidal (simple, some torque ripple) vs sinusoidal FOC (smooth, efficient, complex).

Brushed DC: The H-Bridge

The fundamental power circuit. Four switches let current flow either direction → forward, reverse, brake, coast:

        +V                       Drive table (Q1..Q4):
    ┌────┴────┐                   Forward : Q1+Q4 on
   Q1        Q3                   Reverse : Q2+Q3 on
    │    M    │                   Brake   : Q2+Q4 on (short motor low-side)
   Q2        Q4                   Coast   : all off
    └────┬────┘                   ⚠ Q1+Q2 on = SHOOT-THROUGH (dead short)
        GND

PWM duty cycle sets average voltage → speed. Direction is the bridge state. The cardinal sin is shoot-through: turning on both switches of one leg shorts the supply through them and destroys the MOSFETs in microseconds — prevented by dead-time (a brief both-off gap when switching a leg), generated by the timer’s complementary-PWM hardware.

Stepper: Microstepping

A stepper moves in fixed angular steps by energizing coils in sequence. Microstepping drives the two coils with sinusoidally-weighted currents to interpolate between full steps for smoother, finer motion:

  Coil A current:  sin(θ)     Full step → coarse, loud
  Coil B current:  cos(θ)     1/16 microstep → smooth, quiet, less torque/step

Most steppers run open-loop (no position feedback — you trust that it didn’t miss a step), which is simple but loses position silently under overload. Adding an encoder (sensors) closes the loop.

BLDC & FOC

For a 3-phase motor you drive a 3-phase inverter (three half-bridges, six switches). The control hierarchy:

Trapezoidal (6-step)

Energize two of three phases at a time, commutating through six states per electrical revolution based on rotor position from Hall sensors (or sensorless back-EMF zero-crossing). Simple, but torque ripples at the commutation points.

Field-Oriented Control (FOC)

The high-performance approach: continuously control the motor’s current vector so the magnetic field is always 90° ahead of the rotor (maximum torque per amp), producing smooth, efficient, quiet operation. FOC runs a tight loop (often 10–20 kHz, synced to PWM):

   Measure phase currents (ADC, synced to PWM)
        │
   Clarke transform   3-phase (Ia,Ib,Ic) → 2-axis stationary (Iα,Iβ)
        │
   Park transform     stationary (Iα,Iβ) → rotor frame (Id,Iq)   ← needs rotor angle
        │
   PI controllers     drive Id→0, Iq→torque setpoint
        │
   Inverse Park       back to stationary frame
        │
   SVPWM              → PWM duty for 3 legs (Space-Vector PWM)
        └─── repeat every PWM period

The Clarke/Park transforms are DSP sin/cos math run every loop — exactly why FOC lives on MCUs with an FPU or CMSIS-DSP, often with the loop placed in TCM for zero jitter. The rotor angle comes from an encoder/resolver/Hall, or is estimated (sensorless FOC) from the measured currents and voltages.

Sensing

Position (fine)Quadrature encoder (timer encoder mode), resolver, or magnetic absolute encoder over SPI
SpeedDifferentiate position, or measure encoder pulse frequency

The ADC must sample current at the right instant in the PWM cycle (when the low-side switch is on and the shunt sees phase current) — which is why the timer triggers the ADC, not software.

Where this connects

  • PWM — the fundamental drive signal; complementary PWM + dead-time is the power-stage interface.
  • Timers — generate center-aligned PWM, dead-time, encoder counting, and ADC trigger timing.
  • ADC — current and position sensing, sampled in sync with PWM.
  • DMA — streams ADC samples into the control loop without CPU overhead.
  • DSP & Fixed-Point — Clarke/Park/SVPWM transforms; CMSIS-DSP motor-control functions.
  • Cache & TCM — the FOC loop often runs from ITCM for deterministic timing.
  • Interrupts — the control loop fires from the PWM/timer update interrupt.

Pitfalls

  1. Shoot-through. Both switches in a leg on at once = dead short, instant MOSFET death. Always use hardware dead-time; never drive complementary PWM without it.
  2. ADC sampled at the wrong PWM instant. Reading current when the shunt doesn’t carry phase current gives garbage. Trigger the ADC from the timer at the correct point.
  3. Control loop too slow / jittery. Torque ripple, instability, audible whine. Run FOC at the PWM rate with deterministic timing (TCM, tight ISR).
  4. Wrong rotor angle in FOC. Park transform with a misaligned/offset encoder → no torque or runaway. Always run an alignment/offset-calibration step at startup.
  5. Missed steps (open-loop stepper). Overload silently loses position with no error. Add an encoder or generous torque margin.
  6. No current/over-temperature limit. A stalled motor draws locked-rotor current and cooks the power stage. Implement fault/overcurrent shutdown (many gate drivers have hardware fault pins).
  7. Ignoring back-EMF on shutdown. Suddenly de-energizing an inductive motor produces voltage spikes; provide flyback/freewheeling paths and don’t disconnect under load abruptly.
  8. Floating-point FOC without an FPU. Soft-float transforms can’t keep up at 20 kHz. Use an FPU or fixed-point CMSIS-DSP.
