# Motor Control

## Overview

Motor control is where an MCU stops just reading sensors and starts moving the physical world — and it ties together more peripherals than almost any other embedded task: [PWM](pwm.md) to drive the power stage, [timers](timers.md) for precise switching and dead-time, [ADC](adc.md) for current/position sensing, [DMA](dma.md) to stream samples, [interrupts](interrupts.md) for the control loop, and [DSP](dsp.md) math for the transforms. This page maps the landscape — brushed DC, stepper, and BLDC/PMSM motors — and the control techniques from simple PWM speed control up to **Field-Oriented Control (FOC)**, the algorithm behind quiet, efficient drones, e-bikes, and appliances.

```
   MCU                    Gate driver     Power stage      Motor
   ┌──────────┐  PWM ──▶  ┌──────────┐    ┌──────────┐    ┌─────┐
   │ control  │           │ level    │──▶ │ MOSFET/  │──▶ │  M  │
   │ loop     │  ◀── ADC  │ shift +  │    │ IGBT     │    └─────┘
   │ (FOC)    │  current  │ dead-time│    │ H-bridge │       │
   └──────────┘  ◀── enc  └──────────┘    └──────────┘ ◀─────┘ position
                  position                              feedback
```

## Motor Types

| Type | How it turns | Drive | Control difficulty |
|------|--------------|-------|--------------------|
| **Brushed DC** | Mechanical commutator | 1 H-bridge, PWM duty = speed | Easy |
| **Stepper** | Discrete steps, energize coils in sequence | 2 H-bridges, microstepping | Easy–medium (usually open-loop) |
| **BLDC** (trapezoidal) | Electronic commutation, 6 steps/elec rev | 3-phase inverter, Hall sensors | Medium |
| **PMSM** (sinusoidal) | Smooth rotating field | 3-phase inverter + **FOC** | Hard |

BLDC and PMSM are physically the same hardware (3-phase permanent-magnet); the difference is how you drive them — 6-step trapezoidal (simple, some torque ripple) vs sinusoidal FOC (smooth, efficient, complex).

## Brushed DC: The H-Bridge

The fundamental power circuit. Four switches let current flow either direction → forward, reverse, brake, coast:

```
        +V                       Drive table (Q1..Q4):
    ┌────┴────┐                   Forward : Q1+Q4 on
   Q1        Q3                   Reverse : Q2+Q3 on
    │    M    │                   Brake   : Q2+Q4 on (short motor low-side)
   Q2        Q4                   Coast   : all off
    └────┬────┘                   ⚠ Q1+Q2 on = SHOOT-THROUGH (dead short)
        GND
```

[PWM](pwm.md) duty cycle sets average voltage → speed. Direction is the bridge state. The cardinal sin is **shoot-through**: turning on both switches of one leg shorts the supply through them and destroys the MOSFETs in microseconds — prevented by **dead-time** (a brief both-off gap when switching a leg), generated by the [timer's](timers.md) complementary-PWM hardware.

## Stepper: Microstepping

A stepper moves in fixed angular steps by energizing coils in sequence. **Microstepping** drives the two coils with sinusoidally-weighted currents to interpolate between full steps for smoother, finer motion:

```
  Coil A current:  sin(θ)     Full step → coarse, loud
  Coil B current:  cos(θ)     1/16 microstep → smooth, quiet, less torque/step
```

Most steppers run **open-loop** (no position feedback — you trust that it didn't miss a step), which is simple but loses position silently under overload. Adding an encoder ([sensors](sensors.md)) closes the loop.

## BLDC & FOC

For a 3-phase motor you drive a **3-phase inverter** (three half-bridges, six switches). The control hierarchy:

### Trapezoidal (6-step)

Energize two of three phases at a time, commutating through six states per electrical revolution based on rotor position from **Hall sensors** (or sensorless back-EMF zero-crossing). Simple, but torque ripples at the commutation points.

### Field-Oriented Control (FOC)

The high-performance approach: continuously control the motor's current *vector* so the magnetic field is always 90° ahead of the rotor (maximum torque per amp), producing smooth, efficient, quiet operation. FOC runs a tight loop (often 10–20 kHz, synced to [PWM](pwm.md)):

```
   Measure phase currents (ADC, synced to PWM)
        │
   Clarke transform   3-phase (Ia,Ib,Ic) → 2-axis stationary (Iα,Iβ)
        │
   Park transform     stationary (Iα,Iβ) → rotor frame (Id,Iq)   ← needs rotor angle
        │
   PI controllers     drive Id→0, Iq→torque setpoint
        │
   Inverse Park       back to stationary frame
        │
   SVPWM              → PWM duty for 3 legs (Space-Vector PWM)
        └─── repeat every PWM period
```

The Clarke/Park transforms are [DSP](dsp.md) sin/cos math run every loop — exactly why FOC lives on MCUs with an FPU or [CMSIS-DSP](dsp.md), often with the loop placed in [TCM](cache_tcm.md) for zero jitter. The rotor angle comes from an encoder/resolver/Hall, or is *estimated* (sensorless FOC) from the measured currents and voltages.

## Sensing

| What | How |
|------|-----|
| **Current** | Shunt resistor + amplifier into [ADC](adc.md), sampled in sync with PWM (mid-pulse), often via [DMA](dma.md) triggered by the timer |
| **Position (coarse)** | Hall sensors (3 digital lines, 6 states/rev) |
| **Position (fine)** | Quadrature encoder (timer encoder mode), resolver, or magnetic absolute encoder over [SPI](spi.md) |
| **Speed** | Differentiate position, or measure encoder pulse frequency |

The ADC must sample current at the right instant in the PWM cycle (when the low-side switch is on and the shunt sees phase current) — which is why the [timer](timers.md) triggers the ADC, not software.

## Where this connects

- [PWM](pwm.md) — the fundamental drive signal; complementary PWM + dead-time is the power-stage interface.
- [Timers](timers.md) — generate center-aligned PWM, dead-time, encoder counting, and ADC trigger timing.
- [ADC](adc.md) — current and position sensing, sampled in sync with PWM.
- [DMA](dma.md) — streams ADC samples into the control loop without CPU overhead.
- [DSP & Fixed-Point](dsp.md) — Clarke/Park/SVPWM transforms; CMSIS-DSP motor-control functions.
- [Cache & TCM](cache_tcm.md) — the FOC loop often runs from ITCM for deterministic timing.
- [Interrupts](interrupts.md) — the control loop fires from the PWM/timer update interrupt.

## Pitfalls

1. **Shoot-through.** Both switches in a leg on at once = dead short, instant MOSFET death. Always use hardware dead-time; never drive complementary PWM without it.
2. **ADC sampled at the wrong PWM instant.** Reading current when the shunt doesn't carry phase current gives garbage. Trigger the ADC from the timer at the correct point.
3. **Control loop too slow / jittery.** Torque ripple, instability, audible whine. Run FOC at the PWM rate with deterministic timing ([TCM](cache_tcm.md), tight ISR).
4. **Wrong rotor angle in FOC.** Park transform with a misaligned/offset encoder → no torque or runaway. Always run an alignment/offset-calibration step at startup.
5. **Missed steps (open-loop stepper).** Overload silently loses position with no error. Add an encoder or generous torque margin.
6. **No current/over-temperature limit.** A stalled motor draws locked-rotor current and cooks the power stage. Implement fault/overcurrent shutdown (many gate drivers have hardware fault pins).
7. **Ignoring back-EMF on shutdown.** Suddenly de-energizing an inductive motor produces voltage spikes; provide flyback/freewheeling paths and don't disconnect under load abruptly.
8. **Floating-point FOC without an FPU.** Soft-float transforms can't keep up at 20 kHz. Use an FPU or fixed-point [CMSIS-DSP](dsp.md).
